Energy Flashes

September/October 1983

"THE LAND OF THE RISING SUN" REALLY IS: According to a study by the Worldwatch Institute (Dept. TMEN, 1776 Massachusetts Avenue N.W., Washington, D.C. 20036), some 3.6 million Japanese households-about 11% of the country's homes-were using solar systems (in most cases, hot water) by the end of last year. Furthermore, the government-which backs the alternative technology by offering homeowners easy-to-obtain cash grants that cover half the cost of buying and installing solar heaters-expects the number of solar-equipped buildings to reach 4.2 million by 1985 ... and 8 million by 1990!

THE LARGEST WIND GENERATOR CURRENTLY PLANNED will be installed by Hawaiian Electric Company on Oahu's north coast, near Kahuku, beginning next year. The 7.3 megawatt, $14.2 million machine, designed by General Electric, is expected to be operational by 1985 ... and could supply electricity for as many as 3,500 households, at a cost-it is hoped-of less than five cents per kilowatt-hour.

 NUCLEAR OPINION: In a recent Harris poll, 51% of the people surveyed said they are opposed to the construction of new nuclear power plants (37% are in favor) ... 54% believe that such facilities are dangerous (41% think the reactors are safe) ... 75% are concerned about airborne radioactive emissions ... and 74% view radioactive waste as "a major problem".

 YES, YOU CAN BURN COAL IN CATALYTIC COMBUSTER-EQUIPPED wood/coal combination stoves, according to research announced by Fred Noll, senior product engineer for Corning Glass Works. Noll says that the sulfur in coal coats-but does not destroy-the combuster ... and that burning an 1100Â°F wood fire for one hour will remove the residue. You'll have to leave the combuster bypass open when burning the black chunks, though, or the cleaner may become seriously clogged. Only when burning wood can you actually use your unit's catalytic cleaner to increase heater efficiency and reduce exhaust emissions.

NUKE KNOWLEDGE: The Nuclear Control Institute (Dept. TMEN, 1000 Connecticut Avenue N.W., Washington, D.C. 20036) offers two informative publications for anyone interested in the use and misuse of the "peaceful" atom. Nuclear Control Index of Congress ($2.00 postpaid) records how your Representatives have voted on domestic and International nuclear issues. Breeders, Plutonium, and Nuclear Weapons ($1.00 postpaid) discusses the potential that commercial breeder-type reactors (which are now being developed both in this country and abroad) have for producing weapons-grade plutonium for nuclear warheads.

 A PROJECT DESIGNED "TO HELP AN ENTIRE COUNTRY CHANGE ITS ENERGY HABITS" has been announced by Volunteers In Technical Assistance (VITA). The organization has earmarked $3.4 million for a four-year project to develop prototype alternative energy technologies-including passive solar, photovoltaic, and wind-on a widespread basis in the tiny East African country of Djibouti (population 300,000). It is hoped that the energy-poor nation may someday serve as a model for renewable energy development throughout the Third World.

 A COMPUTERIZED SAILING SHIP: That's what The Oilman, an "ordinary" tanker operating in the North Sea, will become when outfitted with rigid sails that will be automatically controlled by a microprocessor to take maximum advantage of wind conditions. The $247,000 computerized setup will "tie" the sails into the ship's existing conventional power system ... and is expected to save the vessel's owners about $100,000 per year in fuel costs.

 21.9% LESS ENERGY WAS NEEDED TO MAKE A POUND OF ALUMINUM IN 1982 than in 1972, according to the Aluminum Association, Inc., a trade organization. In just the second half of last year, claims the group, more than 90 trillion Btu were saved in the manufacture of the metal, compared with the same period a decade ago. Perhaps most significantly, at least 35% of the reduction is attributed to consumer recycling programs. Recycling aluminum, says the association, saves approximately 95% of the energy required to produce the material from ore.

 MEXICAN BEAN BEETLES THRIVE IN SULFUR DIOXIDE-POLLUTED AIR, according to researcher Patrick Hughes of the Boyce Thompson Institute in Ithaca, New York. Hughes fumigated test plots of soybeans with air containing just 0.14 ppm of SO 2 (the federal standard for the environment is 0.5 ppm) and then placed Mexican bean beetle larvae on the treated patches and on untreated plants. The insects munching on fumigated foliage grew larger than the others ... and the females in the polluted population actually produced twice as many eggs as their "clean" cousins!

IN 1982. U.S. NUCLEAR REACTORS PRODUCED ONLY 56% of their rated capacity, for the fourth year in a row of under-60% performances.... THE SUN WAS THE STAR OF THE TV SERIES "KNOTS LANDING" recently, when a photovoltaic generator was used to film one episode.... TWO WORKING HYDROELECTRIC GENERATORS THAT USE ONLY THE CURRENT FLOWING THROUGH CITY WATER MAINS have been installed in Philipsburg, Montana.... 9,750 SOLAR KING COLLECTORS OCCUPYING 7.7 ACRES will make up the world's largest solar heating system, to be built this year for Packerlanct Packing Company in Green Bay, Wisconsin.... Over the next six years, the Tactical Air Command of the U.S. Air Force will spend $44 million on solar projects, and EXPECTS SUN POWER TO SUPPLY 25% OF ALL THE ENERGY USED AT TAC BASES.... Meanwhile, if the results of reliability tests on contracted Solarex photovoltaic panels prove satisfactory, THE U.S. COAST GUARD WILL CONVERT HALF OF ITS LIGHTED NAVIGATIONAL AIDS TO PHOTOVOLTAIC POWER within three to five years.
