WATER AND THE PERIPHERAL CANAL

Water is dear in California. The scarcity of the liquid - coupled with the expense of transporting it over long distances - has created frequent conflicts throughout the West . . . and the most recent such battle, in the Golden State, centered on Proposition 9.

That initiative would have diverted much of northern California's water to the southern part of the state, by way of a proposed canal that would stretch across the Sacramento River Delta to the California Aqueduct. And although 60% of the relatively few "southerners" who voted approved the measure, northern Californians turned out in overwhelming numbers ... and cast their ballots nine-to-one against , thereby defeating the plan.

Opponents of the Peripheral Canal charged that the massive drainage project would damage the ecology of the delta and of San Francisco Bay . . . and cost the taxpayers a fortune ( billions !), to boot. Furthermore, many voters believed that the south didn't really need the water. And some agribusinesses - which were among those who stood to gain the most from the project, since 85% of the state's water is used for agriculture - joined forces with the opposition, but for a different reason: They hoped that a less expensive waterway, with even fewer environmental restrictions attached to it, might be approved later if the Peripheral Canal project could be defeated.

Meanwhile, newspaper editorials in the southern part of the state blamed the low voter turnout on the fact that the NBA championship game between Los Angeles and Philadelphia was played on election night. The Lakers won . . . and the initiative lost.

However, the defeat sets the stage for yet another contest in California. Sponsors of the Water Resource Conservation and Efficiency Act have collected enough signatures to place a state water policy reform bill (the first in 60 years) on the ballot in November. The measure would regulate the transfer of water within the state, protect existing water supplies, and require both urban and agricultural consumers to develop conservation plans.

THE ORGANIC FARMING ACT

Most of MOTHER's readers have long been aware of the advantages of natural farming methods . . . and of the numerous drawbacks of chemical-intensive agriculture. Well, the rising costs of fuel, synthetic fertilizers, and pesticides have begun to add their weight to the wholistic side of the argument, too, and - as a result - organic farming bills (the first ever to be introduced in Congress) have been submitted in both the House and the Senate.

The proposals (Congressman Jim Weaver's HR 5618, known as The Organic Farming Act of 1982, and Senator Patrick Leahy's S 2485) would authorize the Secretary of Agriculture to [1] train volunteers to help family farmers convert to organic methods, [2] initiate related research at six of the country's landgrant universities, [3] distribute information about alternative farming practices through the Cooperative Extension Service, and [4] provide $14.5 million to promote organic agriculture.

At the time of this writing (early July), the House proposal has received the approval of the Subcommittee on Forests, Family Farms, and Energy ... and has moved to the full Agriculture Committee, where its prospects seem good.

THE CLEAN AIR ACT . . . AND DIRTY DEALING

A recent Harris poll reported that 86% of the American people support the Clean Air Act in its present form . . . and that almost half the population believe the legislation should be even tougher.

And by and large, the act - which became law in 1970 - has been a success. Carbon monoxide in the air declined 33%, sulfur dioxide 20%, and suspended particulates 7% between 1973 and 1978. Furthermore, in 1980 the President's Council on Environmental Quality reported that the act saves 14,000 lives and generates $21 billion in new jobs each year.

Unfortunately, the Clean Air Act is under attack in Congress . . . and if the currently proposed amendments to the law are approved, the country's air quality is likely to deteriorate substantially.

When supporters of a bill designed to strengthen the Clean Air Act submitted the measure to the House Subcommittee on Health and the Environment, a coalition of Republicans and Democrats voted the proposal down . . . and then replaced it with an amendment that would weaken the law.

Ohio Representative Thomas Luken's HR 5252 (nicknamed the "Dirty Air Bill" by its opponents) includes provisions that would, for example, postpone deadlines for compliance with minimum air-quality standards, permit increased levels of air pollution in national parks and other "Class 1" areas, and weaken control standards for vehicle emissions. At the same time, Luken and his cohorts made no attempt to address the issue of controlling toxic pollutants not yet covered by the Clean Air Act, and - much to the annoyance of Canadian as well as U.S. citizens - they totally ignored the increasingly pressing problem of acid precipitation.

Fortunately, when the proposal reached the full Energy and Commerce Committee, defenders of the Clean Air Act managed to reinstate the 1987 deadline for compliance with ozone and carbon monoxide standards . . . and voted to retain the original definition of "pristine air" as worded in the 1970 law. But these were only minor victories, and the Luken bill continues to make headway . . . toward a victory that could well have serious health consequences for every citizen.

POLLUTION . . . THE MILKY WAY

In March, the Ala Moana Canal in downtown Honolulu turned white with 80,000 gallons of chemical-tainted milk . . . which was dumped into the sewer system by Meadow Gold Dairies (the company claims the incident was accidental). The wasted food - which left greasy, sour-smelling clots and thousands of dead fish floating in the waterway - was contaminated with heptachlor . . . a dangerous chlorinated-hydrocarbon pesticide that is now seriously jeopardizing the future of the Aloha State's dairy industry, not to mention the health of the islands' citizens.

Although the Environmental Protection Agency prohibits the use of heptachlor in the U.S., Hawaiian pineapple growers are exempt from the ban, and spray their crops with the compound to control ants and mealybugs. Unfortunately, in the past they've often sold the leaf trimmings from the plants to dairy farmers . . . who in turn fed the foliage-known as "green chop"-to their cows.

The state's milk producers, then, have been unknowingly poisoning their cattle with heptachlor - which accumulates in the animals' systems - and now find that the pesticide has reached toxic levels in many of their dairy beasts. (In some instances, products from Hawaiian bovines have been found to contain 15 times the EPA's allowable limit of such residues.)

Two months after the contaminated milk was detected in the state, the Hawaiian Department of Health recalled many dairy foods . . . including whole and low-fat milk, half-and-half, buttermilk, yogurt, and cottage cheese. But quantities of the tainted products had already been consumed by the public . . . including nursing mothers, several of whom now show excessive levels of heptachlor in their own milk. The dairy farmers, meanwhile, have filed a suit against the pineapple growers . . . and the state's Attorney General is investigating the affair.
