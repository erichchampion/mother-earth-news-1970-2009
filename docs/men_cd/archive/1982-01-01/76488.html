The Wisdom Of Helen And Scott

January/February 1982

As we've noted several times in these pages, Helen and Scott Nearing are light years ahead of most of us when it comes to getting back to the land and living a life of voluntary simplicity. As well they should be, since they originally homesteaded a run-down farm in Vermont's Green Mountains away back in the autumn of 1932.

Life was good for the Nearings on that mini-farm . . . until the slopes around them exploded into ski resorts in the early 50's, forcing Helen and Scott to move on to a rocky inlet on the coast of Maine and start all over again.

And that's where you'll find the Nearings today: still clearing brush, still building honest stone houses (Helen and Scott are famous for their stone houses) and still raising most of their vegetarian diet themselves in unbelievably productive wholistic gardens . . . just as they've been doing for nearly 50 years.

Naturally (in more ways than one), the Nearings have learned a few things about homesteading and getting back to basics over the years. And, lucky for all of us, they've agreed to share some of that knowledge with MOTHER's readers in a regular question and answer column. If you'd like to get in on the action, send your question or questions about self-sufficient living on the land to Helen and Scott Nearing, THE Mother Earth News(restricted), P.O. Box 70, Hendersonville, N.C. 28791. And please don't expect personal replies to your queries. The most important and most frequently asked questions will be answered here-and here only-where we all can read what the Nearings have to say.

I'm interested in vegetarianism, and would like to ask you a few questions about your daily diet. First, how many calories and grams of protein do you each consume to meet your nutritional needs, and do you advise taking vitamin supplements? Second, I feel that a great deal of excess time, effort, and energy is spent in food preparation in most homes. Would you recommend that human beings eat as much of their food as possible in its natural uncooked state, or do you feel that there are some actual benefits derived from cooking certain foods? And, finally, are there any common garden vegetables that you believe shouldn't be eaten raw?

We do not count calories or grams of protein, but we feel that-in general-the American public is overproteinized and too calorie conscious. By simply eating (and not overeating) fresh, unprocessed vegetables combined with whole grains and nuts, an individual ought to be able to obtain all the nutrients he or she needs without taking vitamin supplements.

Your second query, which concerned simplicity in food preparation, is dealt with in Helen's latest book, Simple Food for the Good Life (available for $12.95-plus 95 cents shipping and handling-from Mother's Bookshelf, P.O. Box 70, Hendersonville, North Carolina 28791). The chapters are devoted to such topics as "To Cook or Not to Cook: Raw Versus Cooked Food", "The Work of Cooking: Need It Be Drudgery?", and "To Kill or Not to Kill: Flesh Foods Versus Plants".

And as to garden vegetables that shouldn't be eaten raw . . . there aren't any that we know of. As noted in Helen's cookbook, "There is no substitute for the raw natural food of nature. Boiling, baking, frying, cooking, freezing, drying, salting away, storing, and generally preserving food are poor substitutes for eating things fresh and raw." [EDITOR'S NOTE: Some parts of plants which might be included in your garden crop-such as the leaves of rhubarb and potatoes-are, of course, poisonous . . . whether eaten raw or cooked.]

You stated, in MOTHER NO. 63, that the problems of a day-to-day rural existence make homesteading difficult for a single person to tackle . . . so I'd like to find out your opinions about cooperating on a community level. Do you believe in the age-old practice of working with your neighbors on, for example, building or plowing chores . . . or do you feel that a back-to-the-land couple should try to do it all on their own? Do you now pool your energy-or have you done so in the past-with your neighbors to complete labor-intensive projects? I'd like to know, too, whether you belong to a food co-op where you can obtain staple products at a reduced cost.

We definitely believe in cooperating on a community level and have always tried to offer an extra hand to our neighbors when they've needed it. In our home in Vermont-for instance-we helped with sugaring, wood chopping and sawing, building, and gardening . . . and, in return, we were aided in our larger projects. Homesteaders can, and should, contribute their energies to help their neighbors.

Yes, we do belong to a local food co-op where we buy our staple items. (Of course, such cooperative buying programs weren't much in evidence when we started our move back to the land almost half a century ago.)

My husband and I-both in our late fifties-have purchased a home in the Adirondack Mountains of upstate New York, where we intend to spend our retirement years homesteading. Of course, as part of our move back to a simple lifestyle, we plan to recycle all of our organic kitchen waste for use in the garden. You mentioned (in MOTHER NO. 71) that you compost your household garbage in outdoor piles. However, we wondered if you use any other sort of recycling system in the winter, when cold temperatures slow down the decomposing action in outdoor compost heaps. I seem to recall that composting can be done in large plastic waste cans that are stored in the cellar (or a similar frostproof area). Do you use this method in the cold months . . . or can you suggest an alternative?

We usually build and add to our compost piles during the months when the land is not in "deep freeze". Then, over the course of the winter, we simply carry our garbage out to an in-the-works compost heap and leave it as is until the weather becomes milder. We also have a Clivus Multrum (sometimes called a Swedish "earth closet") composting toilet into which we can throw some of our household wastes, thus saving us from an occasional nipping walk through the snow during a winter storm. We've never stored our compost for any length of time in cans in the cellar.

I realize that the everyday tasks of maintaining a homestead must keep you both in good physical shape, but do you participate in any structured form of exercise (yoga or the martial arts, for instance) to "fine-tune" your bodies?

No, we find that our day-to-day chores on the farm provide us with all the workout our bodies need . . . and we do no formal exercises.

I read your column with interest, and I'm indebted to you both for so generously sharing the knowledge you've gained over the years. MOTHER NO. 71 mentioned that the interior walls of your home are paneled with pine that has been stained permanently. What kind of finish did you use on the wood to pre. serve its natural beauty and at the same time protect it? Also, how do you care for your wooden eating utensils . . . do you use any oil or finish on them?

We decided on a semitransparent oil-based stain (which is sold in various shades from golden yellow to tan) to finish and preserve our paneling. The lighter shades are best-we think-since the wood itself tends to darken naturally as it ages. (We know of folks who stained with a rich shade of brown, but-within a year-their wood became too dark to be attractive.)

We always wash our wooden bowls and spoons-immediately after using them-in warm, lukewarm, or cold running water and then stack the utensils to dry. We use no soap on the wooden implements. Our system must work, because we're still eating from wooden bowls that we purchased over 40 years ago! The only special care we give them is to coat the wood with olive, soy, or peanut oil when it begins to look dry and thirsty.
