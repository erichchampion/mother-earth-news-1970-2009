Friends Of The Earth

January/February 1984

WATT WAS WRONG

Secretary of the Interior James Watt, the Reagan Administration's answer to comedian Don Rickles, finally put his foot in his mouth once too often. As nearly everyone knows, when Watt called his coal-leasing commissioners "a black, a woman, two Jews, and a cripple", Congress called for his resignation.

Like an evil wind that leaves a trail of torn and troubled landscape in its path, Hurricane Watt's legacy will be with us for years to come. Long after his simplistic partisan views and outrageous verbiage have faded from memory, environmentalists-the people whom the Secretary dubbed a "left-wing cult"-will still be working to right Watt's wrongs.

Under James Watt, federal lands were thrown open to exploitation, auctioned off for development, or even suggested as convenient dumping sites for toxic materials and nuclear wastes. Some of the damage the man has wreaked is irreversible . . . but other battles continue to rage.

Environmentalists' efforts to corral an oil and gas drilling stampede in U.S. coastal waters is one struggle that won't halt with Watt's departure. H.R. 3864 will, if passed, protect the California and New England coasts from drillers until the year 2000. And the continued wrestling match over coal leasing-the issue that proved to be the Secretary's undoing-has heightened public and congressional interest in federal land-use practice.

In April 1982, Watt tripped the alarms when he sold 1.6 billion tons of publicly owned coal in Montana and Wyoming for only 3-1/2 cents per ton. At the time, America's "real estate agent" bragged that he was about to lease more coal in one year than had been let out in the previous 62 years. This past September 20, however, the Senate repudiated him by passing the Bumpers-Jackson coal-leasing moratorium by a two-to-one margin. Typically, the Secretary responded by accusing the Senate of lacking the "resolve" to "defend freedom throughout the world".

No, James Watt will not be missed.

WATT'S NEXT?

Columnist George Will offered what may prove to be the best epitaph for the former Secretary of the Interior. "There are only two things wrong with Jim Watt," Will cracked, "his style and his substance." But while Watt is now out of style in Washington, his substance remains.

The successor to the Department of the Interior's top chair, William Clark, originally promised "no change" in the department's current policies. In addition, the strongest qualification that Mr. Clark can claim is that he's a rancher. (Need we mention that President Reagan owns a ranch, too?) Still, lately he has been inviting critics from environmental camps to drop by and talk things over.

Clearly, William Clark will bear close watching.

RUCKELSHAUS ARREST

A new face is certainly no guarantee of a new policy. Remember when William Ruckleshaus was loudly applauded as the white knight who would redeem the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) from the scandals of the Ann Gorsuch-Burford/Rita Lavelle era? Following a cry of alarm in July from the National Academy of Sciences that the acid rain threat needed immediate attention, the new EPA Director promised a plan of action by September. On October 24, Ruckleshaus made his announcement: The recommendations were being put off "indefinitely". Whoops . . . the administration that had promised the new director a "free hand" in "cleaning up the EPA" had just decided to slap on the handcuffs!

UMBRELLA ORGANIZATION

Taking a more active stance on the same issue, 50 people from 40 U.S. and Canadian organizations met for four days in Grand Portage, Minnesota in mid-September to form the U.S. Acid Rain Network. Members include farmers, soil and water conservationists, Native Americans, environmentalists, and people from the fishing and tourism industries. The network plans to issue Damage/Solution Reports "adaptable to every region of the country" as well as "a nice long list of jobs that individuals and organizations can do to address the problem". For more information, contact The North American Water Office, 1519-A East Franklin Avenue, Minneapolis, Minnesota 55404,612/872-1097.

DIOXIN: A BURNING ISSUE

Though they may help rid city dumps of trash overloads, municipal incinerators may also be filling city air with deadly dioxins. This warning comes from EPA researcher Dr. Michael Gross, who claims that urban trash burners may be the second largest source of the deadly compound in the nation. In one incinerator in Texas, for instance, Dr. Gross found dioxin levels in fly ash that were 45 times greater than the established EPA "safe" levels.

Gross was forced to go public with his findings when the information was censored from the EPA report he prepared.

NUKELY ERRORS

The year 1982 was a particularly busy one for paper pushers in the nuclear power industry . . . because power plants spent a lot of extra time reporting to the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) on more than 4,500 safety mishaps. Ten plants chalked up impressive scores of over 100 mishaps each. And 250 of the year's incidents were called "particularly significant" events. (This description refers to such "occurrences" as the near misses that could have led to meltdowns at North Carolina's Brunswick 2 and New York's Salem 2 power plants.) Half of the reported problems were laid to equipment failure, 20% to design flaws, and more than 25% to human error. Meanwhile, the number of workers exposed to measurable amounts of radiation in U.S. power plants jumped to 84,322 . . . a 113% increase over that for 1969. [EDITOR'S NOTE: It should be pointed out, however, that-according to the DOE's Monthly Energy Review-electrical production from nuclear power increased more than 200% during the same period.]

HOT COUTURE

With a short announcement in the Federal Register, the NRC recently shut the lid on importation of cloisonné jewelry. It seems the agency has found that uranium salts traditionally used to color the enameled adornments could expose wearers to as much as 4,000 millirems of radiation annually. The result, the NRC calculated, could be two million cases of skin cancer each year!

Unfortunately, though, while uranium-enriched jewelry is now unimportable, tho rium -bearing cloisonné isn't affected by the ruling . . . nor are the hot-uranium adornments already in use in this country.

ORGANIC FARMING BLOCKED

The laudable Agricultural Productivity Act (H.R. 2714 and S. 1128) that we've reported on in previous issues of MOTHER has fallen on hard times. Secretary of Agriculture Block's department continues to stonewall attempts to encourage the pesticide-free farming the bill advocates. The so-called "Organic Farming Act" would establish pilot research programs on organic agricultural practices and has received strong bipartisan backing in both houses of Congress. (The bill currently has 65 sponsors in the House and 16 Senate backers.) In fact, the only opposition seems to come from the department Block heads!

Congressman James Weaver, angered by what he called "zealous efforts to defeat" the bill, wrote Block a letter complaining that "the USDA's determined opposition to the Agricultural Productivity Act is both shocking and confusing". An avalanche of letters to the President and members of Congress could help break the Reagan Administration's "Blockade".
