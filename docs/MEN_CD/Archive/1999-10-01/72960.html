6 Sat urn reaches "opposition" on a Saturday, the day of the week named for Saturn. Opposition means Saturn appears opposite the sun in the sky and thus rises at sunset and is visible all night long. The planet is also the brightest and biggest it has been in almost a quarter-century (telescopes show the rings tilted a generous 20° from edge on). Look for Saturn rising in evening twilight about one handspan at arm's length to the lower left of a much brighter Jupiter. The largest and the second largest planet form a spectacular pair. 15 For the first time since 1973, the U.S. gets to see the planet Mercury "transit" or creep across the face of the sun. This rare event begins right at sunset as seen from the East Coast, earlier the farther west in the U.S. you are. It can only be observed safely by using a telescope equipped with a proper solar filter (between the sun and the main lens or mirror of the telescope, not between the eye and eyepiece) or with a telescope projecting an image of the sun onto some kind of screen (piece of cardboard or other surface). Mercury will appear as a dark dot blacker and probably smaller than many sunspots that will dot the solar face at this time. At this particular transit, the little planet passes very near the northern edge of the sun

SEASON OF THE LEONIDS
Look for the Meteor Storm!

Sometime between midnight and dawn on either November 17 or 18, 1999, folks in at least some regions of the world will look up to see literally thousands of "shooting stars" flaming across the sky. Because these meteors will all appear to radiate out from a point in the constellation Leo, the event is known as a Leonid meteor storm.

A meteor is a streak of light caused when a piece of space dust or rock enters Earth's atmosphere at speeds so great it burns up from the friction. On certain days every year, when Earth passes through the meteoroidal debris left by particular comets, we may see as many as several dozen shooting stars per hour in what is I called a meteor shower. But a few times a century (at most) Earth may go through far denser clouds of particles closer to a comet that is passing or has recently passed. It's on these rare occasions that nightsky watchers may be treated to a, meteor storm.

In 1833 a Leonid meteor storm was so intense, it woke people in their beds in Boston and is remembered as the "Night the Stars Fell on Alabama." In 1966 folks in parts of the Southwest saw the number of Leonids reach, at least for a minute or so, a rate estimated at about 500,000 meteors per hour! One observer said it was like watching "waterfalls" of shooting stars pour down the sides of the sky.

Who knows what wonders this year's storm might bring-and where? Some experts think that Europe will see the peak in the early hours of November 18 and that by the time Leo rises in the U.S. several hours later, the meteor rates may be greatly reduced. But no one can be sure of the exact timing.

Whatever the case, bear in mind that driving a few miles (or few dozen miles) from city light pollution will make a tremendous difference in the number of Leonids seen and the overall splendor of the show.
