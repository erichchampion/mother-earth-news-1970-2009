Energy Flashes

November/December 1982

FEDERAL ENERGY WASTE: A recent Congressional report says that the Reagan Administration has failed to implement numerous recommended low- or no-cost energy-conservation measures in public facilities and, as a result, is wasting tens of millions of dollars. According to John D. Dingell, chairman of the House Energy Committee, energy consumption by the U.S. government-the nation's largest user-rose by 3.4% in fiscal 1981 after having dropped 6.3% during the previous administration's final two years. The report claims that federal conservation efforts have been "stalled to a complete standstill" under Reagan. For a free copy of the 41-page paper, "Wasted Energy Dollars in the Federal Government" (publication 97-HH), call 202/225-0222, or send a self-addressed mailing label to the House Committee on Energy and Commerce, Dept. TMEN, Room B-334, Rayburn House Office Building, Washington, D.C. 20515.

TODAY'S MAIL: TWO LETTERS AND UNCLE FRED? "People and parcels" deliveries may become commonplace in the future if the powers that be listen to James H. Miller-director of Pennsylvania State University's Public Transportation Program-who suggests that rural mail carriers could provide a valuable, fuel-saving, and perhaps profitable service by hauling passengers as well as ordinary postal fare. Miller points out that double-duty dispatch is common in Germany, Sweden, and Finland . . . but hastens to add that, in this country, it would probably be best handled by private carriers, rather than the U.S. Postal Service.

SOLAR GROWTH: Here are some highlights from a revealing study by the Solar Lobby and the Center for Renewable Resources: The amount of energy used to produce one dollar of Gross National Product has decreased by about 6% since 1978 (an indication that the nation is using energy more efficiently) . . . there are now between 60,000 and 80,000 solar homes in the U.S. (there were roughly 500 in 1977) . . . sales of active solar collectors rose from $17 million in 1975 to $400 million in 1981 . . . and, between 1975 and 1980, total combined sales figures for passive and active "suncatchers"-including photovoltaic cells-increased by 155% annually!

ALAS, EVERYTHING'S RELATIVE: Although the aforementioned study observes that Americans have invested an impressive-sounding $10.5 billion in solar technology since the turn of the decade, the report also points out that we spend $80 billion every year for oil . . . and that, in 1980, consumers spent more on lipstick than on solar collectors.

MAKING TWO RIGHTS FROM TWO WRONGS: Last year, Columbia University's Helmut Schulz developed a process called Simplex Coal and Biomass Gasification that-according to the university's Office of Public Information-involves combining municipal wastes with coal to make briquettes . . . which, when burned, produce a pollutant-free fuel gas. Now, Mr. Schulz has reportedly taken the technique a step further: When hazardous substances such as PCB's and toxic heavy metals are included in the briquette "recipe", the process also yields a sandlike silica-aluminum slag, which-it's claimed-encases the residual poisons and can be used as a harmless road-building material!

A MAGNETICALLY LEVITATED TEN-TON TRAIN hurtled along a four-kilometer run at speeds upward of 186 MPH during tests at Miyazaki, Japan earlier this year. Current induced into magnetic coils laid on each side and along the bottom of a U-shaped "track" created a field with the same polarity as that produced by superconduct_ ing magnets in the body of the train itself. The resultant repelling forces lifted, propelled, and guided the fullsize, two-car prototype.

"ENERGY AND THE ELDERLY SOURCEBOOK" is a 60-page guide to state and federal agencies, plus nongovernment organizations, that can help senior citizens weather the rising costs of home heating and cooling. The booklet-which also includes an analysis of the energy-related problems of older people-is free from the American Association of Retired Persons, Dept. TMEN, 1909 K Street N.W., Washington, D.C. 20049.

WHAT PRICE FIREWOOD? According to a survey by the monthly Wood 'n Ener Solid Fuel Journal (Dept. TMEN, P.O. Box 2008, Concord, New Hampshire 03301 . . . subscriptions 24 per year), people in Tennessee generally pay the lowest prices in the country for a seasoned cord of delivered fuel wood: $33 in rural areas and just $40 in urban Chattanooga. Californians, on the other hand, burn the most money per cord: $125 in rural Auburn, and a whopping $245 in Los Angeles.
