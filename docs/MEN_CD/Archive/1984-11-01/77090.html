A SOLAR POWER CONVERTER THAT MAY CUT THE COST OF SUNLIGHT-SPAWNED HOME ELECTRICITY BY AS MUCH AS 90% has been patented by Alvin M. Marks. The device, called the Lepcon, uses submicron antennas on a glass plate to convert sunlight into high-frequency alternating current, which is then changed by a miniature circuit to direct current. The inventor estimates the cost of electricity from the Lepcon at about 500 per watt, as compared to the $5.00 per watt typical of conventional silicon-crystal photovoltaic equipment. Mr. Marks also claims a 75% efficiency rate for his invention (versus a theoretical 20%, and a thus-far-achieved 8% to 10% rate, for standard solar cells). If mass-produced, a 100-square-foot Lepcon roof panel would cost about $1,500 and generate sufficient power for a small household. A photovoltaic array producing the same output would cost approximately $75,000. (The catch, of course, is that the Lepcon is not yet commercially available, and may not be for quite some time.)

 HOW DO YOU TELL PEOPLE TO "KEEP OUT" FOR 100 CENTURIES? Scientists calculate that 10,000 years will have to elapse before the radioactive waste created in the past 30 years by the nuclear power industry will become "safe." In addition to the as-yet-unresolved problems of how and where to store the stuff, there's the question of how to warn hundreds of future generations about the material's hazards and locations. The Department of Energy recently commissioned proposals from scientists in several disciplines, and received a variety of interesting ideas. Thomas Sebeok, a linguistics expert at Indiana University, suggested establishing an "atomic priesthood" that would create and perpetuate myths and taboos about the sites to scare people away. Most of the reports, however, focused on effective communications techniques and suitably enduring markers. Perhaps the most practical was submitted by archaeologist Maureen Kaplan, who, after studying such lasting monuments as the Pyramids and the Acropolis, suggested building Stonehenge-like rings of rock monoliths around the "hot" dumps. The pillars would then be inscribed with pictographic and multilingual warnings discouraging deep drilling and mining.

HIGH-TECH MEETS LOW-TECH: COMPUTERIZED WOOD STOVES may begin appearing on the market in 1985, according to a report in the trade publication Wood 'n Energy. Several manufacturers are currently working on equipment that employs a microprocessor to monitor heat intensity inside a firebox and open or close the draft to achieve a preset room temperature level. In addition to eliminating many of the manual tasks involved in tending a fire, the computerized units could help conserve fuel by burning only as much wood as necessary to keep a home comfortable.

ELECTRIC UTILITY COMPANIES SPENT 40% MORE ON TV ADVERTISING last year than in 1982, says the Television Bureau of Advertising. Commercials sponsored by the U.S. Committee for Energy Awareness, an industry public relations group (see "Energy Flashes," MOTHER N0. 82), accounted for nearly two-thirds of the escalation. The number of ads promoting electric heat pumps and electricity itself increased, while ads encouraging conservation decreased.

LAND-POOR, GADGET-RICH JAPAN MAY BE POISONING ITSELF WITH TINY BATTERIES . Forty percent of Japan's mercury output goes into making the small cylindrical or button-shaped dry cells that power cameras, watches, calculators, and other electronic gizmos. Eventually, of course, those batteries end up in garbage dumps, where they leak mercury into the soil. The problem is compounded by the fact that land is precious in Japan, so waste-disposal sites that are deemed full are often converted to other uses, such as school yards. Electronics shops are now being supplied with collection boxes where customers can return batteries for recycling. (Incidentally, some 30 to 40% of Japan's battery production, which totaled 2.85 billion units in 1983, is exported to the United States and other countries.)
