Energy Flashes

September/October 1984

THE AVERAGE GROSS SALARY EARNED BY A FULL-TIME CHIMNEY SWEEP is $31,494, according to a survey conducted at the National Chimney Sweep Guild's 1983 convention by Wood 'n Energy magazine (P.O. Box 2008, Laconia, NH 03247; annual 12-issue subscription, $24). The study also showed that sweeps work hard to make that money: During the fall and early winter, the typical soot-scrubber works just under 57 hours a week ... and many respondents listed weekly totals in the 80's and 90's. Sweeps seem to be a self-sufficient lot, too: 79% cited independence (not money) as the major reason why they chose to become sweeps ... more than one-fourth of those surveyed said they work alone .. and 6.9% are husband-and-wife teams!

THE NATION'S FIRST UNDERGROUND HYDROELECTRIC POWER PLANT has been built in a ten-story-high chamber, 1,000 feet beneath the Sierra National Forest in California's Sierra Nevada mountains. Water from nearby Courtright Lake flows three miles over a declining below-ground spillway ... plunges 1,500 feet down a vertical tunnel into the facility's turbines ...and then flows out of the mountain to Lake Wishon, three-quarters of a mile away. During off-peak hours, the turbines are reversed and pump the liquid back to Court-right Lake, so that the power-producing process can be repeated the next day.

BY THE TURN OF THE CENTURY, ONLY 52% OF ALL CARS WILL RUN ON GASOLINE , say engineers for Volkswagen of West Germany, while 23% will use methanol, 15% diesel, 6% liquefied petroleum gas, and 3.5% ethanol from biomass. Furthermore, they predict that the steel content of autos will decline from the current 79% to 50% by the year 2000. Plastic and aluminum body parts, along with ceramic engine components, will replace the more conventional material.

THE HIGH PRICE OF NUCLEAR POWER WILL SOON CAUSE FURTHER SUBSTANTIAL RATE HIKES in many parts of the country, according to a survey of regulatory, utility, and state officials conducted by the trade weekly Energy User News. Construction delays, budget overruns, and safety problems were cited as the primary reasons behind the anticipated increases. Some examples: The expected completion of Pennsylvania Power & Light Company's second Susquehanna reactor may boost power costs by as much as 22% ... Louisiana Power & Light is seeking a 51% hike ... Kansas Gas & Electric Company has proposed a five-year plan that would raise rates 39.5% in 1985 and between 8.5 and 10.2% in each of the ensuing four years ... and Union Electric Company in St. Louis has filed for a 70% cumulative increase over a five-year period.

IS MAGMA AN ENERGY SOURCE OF THE FUTURE? The Department of Energy (DOE) and Sandia National Laboratories in New Mexico are studying the feasibility of an ambitious plan to tap into the earth's magma-the molten rock within our planet's interior-to extract thermal energy. According to the U.S. Geological Survey, the energy potential of magma within six miles of the earth's crust in this country is 800 to 8,000 times greater than current annual energy use. The idea: Bore into the material (the temperature of which ranges between 1,500 and 2,000Â°F) and use the heat to convert water or other liquids to turbine-turning steam or gas.

"CHEAP" DIESEL FUEL MAY BE COSTLY IN OTHER WAYS , according to testimony delivered by Bob Yuhnke of the Environmental Defense Fund before the House Subcommittee on Health and the Environment. Yuhnke cited a study showing that diesel truck drivers got 11.9 times more urinary tract cancer than a control group ... and 7.2 times more cancer than drivers of gas-powered trucks. The study also revealed that railroad workers exposed to diesel fumes contracted 42% more respiratory cancer than a control group. Since diesel autos emit 80 to 100 times more particulates than their petrol-driven counterparts, experts are concerned about the consequences of a proliferation of the cars. "If driven in sufficient numbers," said Yuhnke, "diesel vehicle emissions could soon rival cigarettes and drunk drivers as one of the nation's most serious threats to public health."

IN 1982, THE NATION'S TOP 150 UTILITY COMPANIES OVERCHARGED CUSTOMERS $5.1 BILLION for federal income taxes that the firms never paid. That amount is in addition to the $3.7 billion the companies overcharged in 1981 (see MOTHER NO. 85's Energy Flashes). A law adopted by Congress in 1969 allows a utility to charge ratepayers for tax expenses without first figuring in the many tax breaks such a company receives. As a result, the utilities now have about $34 billion in "deferred tax" funds collected over the years ... "the equivalent of $405 for every U.S. household," according to the Environmental Action Foundation (Suite 731, 1346 Connecticut Ave. N.W., Washington, DC 20036). Representative Tom Harkin (D-Iowa) has introduced "The Phantom Tax Reform Act" (HR 4923) in an effort to relieve consumers of the burdensome overcharges ... but the bill faces stiff opposition by the electric companies and their trade association, the Edison Electric Institute.

That's an increase of 33% over 1982 .... 9.2 MILLION CARS IN BRAZIL NOW RUN ON A BLEND OF 20% ETHANOL IN GASOLINE, indicating the success of that country's national program to develop biofuels .... A $7 BILLION HYDROELECTRIC POWER PROGRAM IS UNDERWAY IN ALASKA. The project is intended to relieve the state-and its remote communities in particular-from heavy reliance on fossil fuels .... In an effort to curb the environmental destruction caused by acid rain, CANADA HAS LAUNCHED A PROGRAM TO REDUCE ITS SULFUR DIOXIDE EMISSIONS BY 50% WITHIN A DECADE. Citing a "need for more research," however, the Reagan Administration has refused to act in kind.
