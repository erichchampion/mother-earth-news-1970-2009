AS MUCH AS 10,000 MEGAWATTS OF WIND SYSTEM GENERATING CAPACITY may be installed worldwide by 1990, according to Robin Sacks, an advisor to the United States House Select Committee on Science and Technology. Ms. Sacks says that industry sales totaled $39 million in 1980 and grew to $108 million in 1982, with more than 100 companies around the globe actively involved in designing and/or manufacturing some 200 different wind system products. She also pointed out, however, that the future security of the industry depends largely upon whether or not U.S. firms succeed in bringing the cost of installed wind power-which now ranges from a scant 7d per kilowatt hour to a high of 35d-down to consistently low levels that will be competitive if state and federal tax credits end.

BUYING ENERGY-EFFICIENT HOUSING CAN GET YOU A BETTER MORTGAGE in the Denver-Boulder area of Colorado, thanks to a unique concept that's likely to spread to other regions . . . called the Home Energy Loan Program (HELP). For a $100 fee, a potential buyer can get a complete HELP appraisal of an existing home's energy consumption, and then use the study (if it's favorable) to help parlay a larger-than-usual mortgage from any of several participating lending institutions. Columbia Savings and Loan Association, for example, normally does not approve mortgages that would require payments amounting to more than 26% of the applicant's gross monthly income . . . but the firm will extend the limit to 30% on HELP-approved homes. The logic behind the program, of course, is that a homeowner who pays lower utility bills can afford higher house payments.

GO WITH THE FLOW: The California Energy Commission has calculated that adjusting the timing of traffic signals to eliminate delays or stops at some 20,000 intersections in the state-a project expected to cost about $40 million-will ultimately save Golden State motorists $550 million in vehicle operating costs annually . . . and will conserve some 150 million gallons of gasoline each year.

When researchers at the University of Illinois taught four-week-old piglets to control the heat in their own pens (the animals were trained to hit a special radiant-heater switch with their snouts), the little porkers cut the facility's heating costs by 53%, compared with a similar setup controlled by a conventional thermostat. DOWN-TO-EARTH SOLAR HEAT STORAGE: The folks in Hatfield, Massachusetts hated to see the summer's energy absorbed by 60 solar collectors mounted atop their high school's roof go to waste . . . so they devised a way to "bury" the surplus calories in a huge (100' -long, 85' -wide, and 8' -deep) insulated pit for use later in 'the year. Sun-warmed water is continuously pumped through the dirt "heat sponge", which was expected-as a result-to reach and hold an optimum 180째F by the time school opened this fall. Although not exactly dirt cheap (the project cost $165,000 . . . nearly two-thirds of which was paid by the state energy office and by a local utility company), the unique storage and recovery system should supply as much as 25% of the school's winter heating needs.

 THE WIND IS BLOWING LAND PRICES SKY-HIGH in some regions of California, where tax incentives and strong air currents-not to mention federal regulations that require utilities to buy electricity produced by alternative means-have fostered a proliferation of "wind farms" (see Energy Flashes, MOTHER NO. 80) on otherwise "worthless" acreage. Investors eager to get in on the action are buying desert scrub land and barren mountain passes-not for the property itself, but for the wind blowing across it-at prices three or four times what the turf would've sold for a year or two ago.
