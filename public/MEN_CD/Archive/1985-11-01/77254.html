Energy Flashes

November/December 1985

IT'S NO ACCIDENT THAT BETWEEN 1.7 MILLION AND 8.8 MILLION METRIC TONS OF PETROLEUM ARE SPEWED INTO THE OCEANS EACH YEAR , says a study released by the National Research Council. Although tanker mishaps and offshore operations account for some marine oil pollution-0.4 and 0.05 metric tons a year (mta) respectively-the biggest contributions come from more chronic sources: routine tanker operations (0.7 mta), municipal wastes (0.7 mta), other runoff and wastes (0.5 mta), marine bilge and fuel oils (0.3 mta), and petroleum carried in rainwater or the atmosphere (0.3 mta).

MEANWHILE, THINGS AREN'T ANY BETTER HERE ON DRY LAND: According to an article in Technology Review , more than 1 billion gallons of used lubricants are generated in the United States annually-and about half, some 500 million gallons, are dumped directly into the environment in landfills and through other improper disposal methods. Worse yet, the most common offenders by far are those of us who change the oil in our own automobiles. Of the 350 million gallons of oil drained from crankcases by do-it-yourselfers each year, only about 18% is recycled or burned as fuel. Forty percent is poured directly into the ground, and another 20% is dumped in the garbage (the remaining 22% is essentially unaccounted for). If you're guilty of contributing to the problem, stop now: Some service station operators will gladly take your used crankcase oil and recycle or dispose of it properly.

ALL IS NOT ROSY AT THE WORLD'S LARGEST ETHANOL PRODUCTION PLANT in South Bend, Indiana. As we mentioned in this column in issue 93, the $186 million facility can convert 20 million bushels of corn into more than a million gallons of fuel ethanol each week. Unfortunately, the plant also produces a powerful, offensive odor-a problem that the citizens' group CEASE (Committee of Environmentalists Against the Stench of Ethanol) has no intention of tolerating. While New Energy Company of Indiana, the plant's owner, claims that measures to curb the smell would cost $4 to $5 million-a price the firm says it can't afford-CEASE points to the odor's detrimental effect on South Bend's environment and economy, and is threatening legal action.

NO SIMPLE FUNERAL FOR THE COUNTRY'S FIRST ATOMIC POWER PLANT: In 1982, after a quarter century of operation, the Shippingport Nuclear Reactor-the first such generating facility in the U.S.-was shut down. After considerable deliberation over how to dispose of the radioactive pressurized-water reactor, DOE has decided on a somewhat elaborate funeral rite. Sometime in mid-1989, the pressure vessel (which contains the "hot" core) will be lifted by a crane, encased in concrete, and loaded onto a barge on the Ohio River. From there, the 770-metric-ton corpse will travel to the Gulf of Mexico, through the Panama Canal, and up the West Coast to its final resting place: a large pit at the nuclear waste disposal site at Hanford, Washington. Total cost: $100 million. And not incidentally, 15 more U.S. reactors are due to be similarly decommissioned by the year 2000.

HOW MUCH HAVE CARBON DIOXIDE LEVELS IN THE ATMOSPHERE CHANGED SINCE THE INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTION? To answer that question, scientists at the University of Bern in Switzerland studied ice core samples taken from 200 meters below the surface of western Antarctica. Their findings: Around the year 1750, atmospheric CO, levels amounted to about 280 parts per million. In 1984, CO, concentrations measured about 345 ppm-a whopping 22% increase, and a disturbing indication that predictions of the greenhouse effect (a global warming trend caused by the trapping of heat by carbon dioxide) could be all too accurate.

OPEC MAY BE FLOUNDERING NOW, BUT IT'LL BE STRONGER THAN EVER IN THE NINETIES if the current oil glut causes a reversal of conservation trends, according to a report by The Worldwatch Institute (1776 Massachusetts Ave. N.W., Washington, D.C. 20036). Although increased oil production by non-OPEC countries-particularly Mexico, Great Britain, and the U.S.-has helped to weaken the OPEC cartel, the stage is being set for another turning of the tables. "OPEC is producing only half as much oil as it did four years ago," says Christopher Flavin, author of the study, "while outside OPEC, oil reserves are being drained more quickly than ever." Noting that Middle Eastern OPEC nations have 56% of the world's oil, Flavin points out that "at the 1984 extraction rate, U.S. oil reserves would last only nine years, while Saudi reserves would last 100 years." Ironically, today's plummeting petroleum prices-due in large part to a decade of energy awareness and vastly improved efficiency-are tending to discourage investment in renewable energy technologies, worsening the picture for the decades to come.

FEDERAL INCOME TAX CREDITS FOR CONSERVATION AND RENEWABLE ENERGY EQUIPMENT END ON
DECEMBER 31. Despite repeated efforts to gain an extension of the credits-which have been largely responsible for the growth of today's renewable energy industry and have enabled thousands of Americans to reduce their consumption of electricity and fossil fuels-the tax incentives created as part of the National Energy Act of 1978 will cease at the end of this year.
