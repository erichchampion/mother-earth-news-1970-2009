Energy Flashes

July/August 1984

A 1-MEGAWATT "WINDWALL" IS PLANNED FOR CALIFORNIA'S ALTAMONT PASS. A double strand of 70-kilowatt turbines, strung along a prestressed cable, will be stretched across a valley site to catch incoming winds. According to its designers, the innovative system will capture six to ten times as much energy as conventional tower-mounted machines in the same amount of space ... and will save money on support structures, as well.

CHEMICAL SOUP INDEED! CYANIDE, URANIUM, MERCURY, AND PLUTONIUM -and at least 136 other dangerous chemicals and radioactive substances-were found in creek sediments near Oak Ridge, Tennessee, during a study conducted for the Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA). The Department of Energy's (DOE) nearby Y-12 plant, which produces enriched uranium components for nuclear weapons, is believed to be the primary source of the contamination. DOE is said to be "analyzing" the report.

IF YOU SEE WHAT APPEARS TO BE A VERY LARGE PLAYING CARD ON WHEELS rolling down a western highway, it's probably just Joel Davidson or Greg Johanson in their photovoltaic-powered speedster. Using 24 extra light 43-watt Arco Solar PV panels-mounted flat on a horizontal platform-and a 1-horsepower electric motor, the vehicle is expected to be capable of reaching unprecedented solar speeds of between 38 and 45 MPH in full sun!

SAY ''CHEESE'' AND TEAR UP YOUR UTILITY BILLS. Foremost Dairies in Le moore, California, has installed a unique system that converts cheese wastes into enough biogas to meet all of the firm's energy needs. Each day, the $2.3 million anaerobic digester will produce 400,000 cubic feet of 70%,to 80% pure methane from whey, a by-product of cheese making. The first-of-its-kind system is odor-free, needs only one-tenth the energy required to operate a conventional (aerobic) plant of equivalent capacity, and is expected to pay for itself in less than two years.

ONE OF THE MOST AMBITIOUS ALTERNATIVE ENERGY PROJECTS IN THE WESTERN HEMISPHERE has been launched by wartorn Nicaragua's Sandinista government. The destruction of one of the country's main fuel depots-along with 135 million gallons of petroleum-by CIA-backed "contra" forces last fall, and greatly reduced oil shipments to the nation (which produces no oil of its own) have prompted a plethora of projects aimed at energy self-sufficiency. Among them are a nationwide energy conservation and gas-rationing program ... plans for five medium-size (17- to 350-megawatt) hydropower plants ... three major biomass projects, including one to gasify wood and cotton wastes into usable fuel ... and construction of six 35-megawatt geothermal plants (one of which is already operating) to employ the heat from Nicaragua's active volcanoes to produce electricity. The government is focusing specifically on ways to decentralize its energy system so that power is produced on a local basis, close to its users.

ONLY ONE IN TEN LENDERS GIVES BUYERS OF ENERGY-EFFICIENT HOMES PREFERENTIAL CONSIDERATION when evaluating mortgage applications, according to a survey conducted for Owens-Corning Fiberglas Corporation. Nearly six in ten of those interviewed-primarily presidents or vice presidents of banks or savings and loan organizations-said they plan to offer such allowances in the future ... but apparently the officials don't do so now because they believe there's no reliable way to evaluate a structure's efficiency. Recently, however, the Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corporation cited several available measurement programs (such as the National Home Builders' Thermal Performance Guidelines, and the Virginia and Maryland Home Builders' E-7 Program) in an effort to encourage preferential loan treatment. Says Richard Trumbull, vice president of Owen sCorning, ". . . with the exception of a drop in interest rates, recognizing energy efficiency in the mortgage lending process may be the best way to help more Americans qualify for housing."

"MOBILE BIOMASS HARVESTING UNITS" MAY SOMEDAY ROAM THE RANGE of the American Southwest if a plan being studied seriously by the federal government and Arizona State University ever comes to fruition. The machines would scavenge dense scrub and brush, grind the material, and convert the pulp on the spot-through a process known as indirect liquefaction-into diesel fuel. A ton of foraged feedstock would yield 40 to 50 gallons of liquid gas, and the machines would be designed to cut plants above their roots so that the vegetation could grow back to provide still more fuel. The U.S. Forest Service, which often must burn scrub in order to improve grazing land and wildlife habitat (and to control wildfires), sees special promise in the project.

WISCONSIN HAS INSTALLED TWO SOLAR REST STOPS on Interstate 43 near Green Bay, as a result of the success of one such unit set up in 1979 along I-90/94. The privies' collectors provide 50% of the heating and hot water needed.... TIBET'S SOLAR POTENTIAL IS SECOND ONLY TO THE SAHARA'S, say Chinese scientists, so they're planning a wide-ranging program in the land of Shangri-la to utilize the sun for heating sheep pens, growing greenhouse vegetables, and generating electricity...
