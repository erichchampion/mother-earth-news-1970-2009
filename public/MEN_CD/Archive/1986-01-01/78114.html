Energy Flashes...

January/February 1986

FEDERAL TAX BREAKS FOR NONRENEWABLE ENERGY SOURCES AMOUNT TO AT LEAST $26 BILLION A YEAR , says a study by the Environmental Action Foundation. According to the report, credits and loopholes in federal income tax policy cost the country a fortune in revenue that would otherwise be due from hard-path energy industries: over $12 billion for electric utilities . . . $12 to $16 billion for oil and gas firms . . . and $1.75 billion for coal companies. And, says The Solar Lobby, if you include other federal supports given to conventional energy industries-grants, loans, loan guarantees, etc. the total subsidies nearly double: close to $50 billion in 1984 alone. Almost a third of that pie, incidentally, went to nuclear power, although atomic energy produced less than 100,000 Btu per federal dollar spent on it in 1984. Other energy sources-including renewables-returned as much as several million Btu per dollar.

"THE SOLAR SILVER ARROW," A PV-POWERED RACER BUILT BY MERCEDES-BENZ, won first place in Switzerland's 228-mile Tour de Sol road rally. The sleek, fiberglass-over-steel car has some standard Mercedes features-independent suspension, rack-and-pinion steering, and four-wheel disc brakes-plus a not-so-standard 480-watt PV cell array that powers two 1.2 horsepower DC motors and charges a silver-zinc battery. The Silver Arrow averaged 37 mph over the final leg of the five-day event, and is considered a likely contender in the next international solar vehicle meet: the BP Solar Challenge, a 2,000-mile race along Australia's east coast, planned for November 1987.

A 76-STORY SKYSCRAPER THAT HEATS AND COOLS ITSELF USING THE BODY HEAT OF ITS OCCUPANTS and the warmth from interior lighting and office equipment has been built in Seattle, Washington. A system incorporating nearly 2,000 water-source heat pumps was installed in the 1.5 million-square-foot building at a cost of about $14 million-some 40% less than the probable price of a conventional setup. Although electric boilers will switch on automatically when necessary to keep the building comfortable, engineers estimate that the heat generated by people and office fixtures will be sufficient 99% of the time.

DEVELOPMENT OF AN "AER OSPACEPLANE" CAPABLE OF FLYING UP TO FIVE TIMES THE SPEED OF SOUND (a bit over 3,800 mph!) may soon be under way. Powered by hydrogen-fueled, "air-breathing" engines, the new plane, otherwise known as a TAV, or transatmospheric vehicle, would be six times lighter than the space shuttle but capable of delivering payloads up to five times heavier. As a space transport, it would fly to the edge of the atmosphere, where auxiliary rockets would boost it into orbit. Commercial flights would probably be flown within the atmosphere. In either case, the TAV could use a conventional jet runway for takeoff and landing. Speed doesn't come cheap, though: Congress is being asked for $2 billion to fund a prototype, and an operational model, which the Pentagon hopes to have flying by the year 2000, would cost between $5 and $10 billion.

NUCLEAR POWER HAS BEEN OUTLAWED IN D ENMARK . Last year, after more than a decade of debate between the country's antinuclear activists and utility companies, the Danish Parliament voted to approve the following statement: "The Parliament directs the government to adjust the official energy plans to include the condition that nuclear power will not be used. The government shall therefore inform the electric utilities that they will not receive . . . permission to construct nuclear power plants."

THE WATER IN CALIFORNIA'S GLENN-COLUSA IRRIGATION DISTRICT IS PRODUCING POWER AS WELL AS PLANT GROWTH now that engineers have installed a small hydroelectric plant in the system. It's the first hydro facility in the world to operate as part of an irrigation network, and is expected to generate an annual 670,000 kilowatt-hours of electricity . . . which, when sold to the local utility, will yield about $33,000 in additional income for the district.
