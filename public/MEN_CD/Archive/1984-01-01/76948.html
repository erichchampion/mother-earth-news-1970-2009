Energy Flashes

January/February 1984

UTILITIES ARE CHARGING CUSTOMERS BILLIONS OF DOLLARS FOR FEDERAL INCOME TAXES THAT ARE NEVER PAID , according to a study released by the Environmental Action Foundation (EAF). A law written by utility lobbyists and adopted by Congress in 1969 allows an electric company to charge its customers as if it didn't receive tax breaks. But the fact is, combined federal tax subsidies for utilities now total $5 billion to $10 billion a year. The Power Line , EAF's monthly publication (Dept. TMEN, 724 Dupont Circle Building, Washington, D.C. 20036 . . . annual subscription, $25), printed some interesting figures from the survey in a recent issue: In 1981, the nation's top 100 electric companies billed ratepayers for $5 billion in federal income taxes, but reported paying only $1.3 billion to the IRS . . . Commonwealth Edison, for example, charged its customers for an incredible $208 million in federal income taxes, but reported paying only $12 million to the IRS . . . and finally, 17 of the 100 companies surveyed reported paying no federal income taxes at all . (And 16 of those utilities were eligible for tax refunds, but continued to charge ratepayers as if they'd paid taxes. In fact, Public Service Co. of Oklahoma claimed a refund of $22.4 million, and then charged its customers $25.4 million for federal income taxes!)

A WIND-POWERED WATER PUMP THAT WORKS IN WIND SPEEDS AS LOW AS TWO MILES PER HOUR is now being manufactured by Cobden Turbines of Mildura, Victoria in Australia. The 30-bladed rotor mounted at the front of a jet engine-like air scoop is said to be a substantial improvement over conventional pumpers, which require wind speeds of at least eight miles per hour. In addition, the machine can be rotated horizontally to work as a giant defroster for protecting orchards: The blades pump higher, warmer air toward the ground to displace the cold inversion layer that usually accompanies frost.

ELECTRONIC ROAD-USE PRICING IN TRAFFIC-CONGESTED CITIES may become a reality if a pilot program planned for Hong Kong is successful. The scheme involves installing electronic identification plates in some 5,000 government vehicles . . . and burying sensors tuned to the tags beneath 30 of the city's most congested intersections. Whenever a bugged auto or truck passes over a detector, the "underground tollbooth" will send a signal to a central computer, which in turn will calculate the appropriate road-use charge (prices will vary according to the location, time, and day) and add it to the vehicle's account. If the test works, every auto and truck in Hong Kong will be equipped with the tags . . . and drivers will receive road-use bills in the mail each month.

A COAL-POWERED CADILLAC ELDORADO has been developed by GM and is housed at the General Motors Technical Center in Warren, Michigan. The auto runs on finely powdered coal dust that's mixed with air and injected into a firing chamber in small, metered amounts. The car's builders say that the vehicle demonstrates the fuel economy and power of a small conventional V-8, but there are some problems . . . namely, very slow acceleration, engine-damaging particulates, and high levels of nitrogen oxide emissions. Researchers at GM and elsewhere, however, are working to overcome those obstacles.

NUCLEAR POWER IS IN TROUBLE IN EUROPE, TOO: Spain has "indefinitely deferred" construction of five nuclear generating plants, including three that were already partially built at a cost of $2.2 billion. And in France, six planned reactors have been shelved . . . while remaining nuclear power construction in that country is expected to raise utility rates by at least 7% above inflation each year.

BOATS THAT "SWIM" LIKE PORPOISES may be in our future if a new marine propulsion system, demonstrated by engineers at the Tokyo Institute of Technology, proves popular. The scientists installed a horizontal finlike "propeller"a-9" X 30" sheet of flexible aluminum with a rubber edge-on a small motorboat, and connected it to the vessel's engine with a rotary cam and pushrods that move the device up and down like a porpoise's tail. The flipper proved to be 30% more efficient than conventional propellers at speeds under six knots . . . and because it has no whirling blades, it shouldn't cause injuries.

WE'RE GETTING BETTER, BUT . . . Cambridge Energy Research Associates recently reported that more Americans than ever are conserving energy in the winter. Ten years ago, the organization says, 85% of us kept our home interiors at temperatures of at least 70 Â°F, but today only 45% maintain such a high heat setting. And the Energy Information Administration (EIA) has also come out with encouraging news, saying that petroleum consumption in the U.S. during 1982 declined 5% . . . the fourth year in a row such a decrease has been noted. But EIA also pointed out that the average citizen today still consumes 306 million Btu each year: the equivalent of 14 tons of coal, or 53 barrels of oil, per person.

THE PEOPLE'S REPUBLIC OF CHINA is actively seeking contact with firms selling alternative-energy information and equipment, according to China Industrial Development, Inc. (Dept. TMEN, 447 West Garvey Avenue, Suite 130, Monterey Park, California 91754) . . . . British inventor Alan Freeman has developed a PHOTOVOLTAIC-POWERED BICYCLE . A 21-watt solar-cell panel mounted on the handlebars drives the vehicle at speeds up to eight miles per hour in sunshine, and a standard 12-volt battery takes over on cloudy days .... Meanwhile, A SUN-FUELED "SOLAR CAR" HAS CROSSED NORTH AMERICA , making the 2,500-mile trip from Long Beach, California to Daytona Beach, Florida in 18 days. The auto-dubbed "Silent Challenger"-runs directly on photovoltaic-induced current and will race an Australian "solar car" across the United States this June.
